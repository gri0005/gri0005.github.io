What part of the experience did you spend the most time engaging with?

I definitely spent the most time engaging with the soccer game, clicking on the ball and trying to get it into the goals. This is mostly attributable to the fact that I wanted the gratification of getting the ball in the goal, but ended up getting the ball stuck to the side of the goal and had to spend a lot of time getting it positioned back to a place where it was possible to get a goal. I was invested in the game, and I refused to move on until I got a succesful result.

What was the most common action in your two minute interaction with the experience?

The most common action by far was clicking the soccer ball. In general, my interactions with the experience involved a lot of spam clicking and repeated actions, which was rewarded by the satisfying animations.

What is your impression of the intended primary goal of the interactive experience?

Over the course of the interactive experience, I (the user) felt a consistent sense of gratification and reward. It seems that the intended primary goal of this experience is to provide a rewarding experience, which will convince the user to download various apps in order to extend that feeling.

How does the interactive experience communicate this primary goal?

The "discover" or "enter" button in the corner, which leads to a promotional popup for the app, had insistent animations - clearly inviting and enticing the user to click them. The limited text on screen, and the app and phone/laptop elements within each scene also further this agenda.

What is your impression of how the experience should be interacted with over time?

It seems that the experience should be interacted with briefly, for about 10 minutes, maximum. The experience could certainly be interacted with on more than one occassion, but likely no more than a handful of times.

How does the interactive experience communicate how it should be interacted with over time?

In the experience, there is only so much to do. After exploring the few interactive elements in each of the pages, the intended natural conclusion to the experience is downloading the apps, which would mean there's no longer any need to visit the site. At that point, it has served its purpose.

portion 2

What other media forms (digital or otherwise) does the experience reference?

The experience references app media forms, advertisement and video games through design elements and explicit content. There are images of laptops and phones represented in many of the environments, implying the relevance of apps and internet. Apps are also explicitly referenced in the promotional screens, convincing the user to download specific services, turning the whole experience into a big example of advertisement media. Video game design elements are also appropriated, such as the coins and reward animations in certain mini-game animation elements.

What does this reference/s communicate to you about how you should act when engaging with your research experience?

The experience's resemblance to video games communicates to the user that it can and should be experienced interactively. The game connection may also cultivate a mindset of consumption, as the coins are similar to those used prominently in games that feature in-app purchases. Similarly, the advertising elements of the experience inform the user that they are expected to undertake an action, i.e. download an app, if they have enjoyed the experience.

What does this communicate to you about how you should feel when engaging with your research experience?

These elements encourage the user to feel competetive when interacting with gamified elements, gratified when receiving some kind of tokenistic reward from those elements, and motivated to invest (either resources or time) in the product.

What is the most frustrating part of the interaction to you and what makes that part frustrating?

The most frustrating part of the experience is the absence of things - the loading times, the graphic elements on screen that aren't interactive or animated, the elements that are clearly meant to be interactive, but which you can't decipher how they are, or which seem to have no change when you click on them. The high satisfaction of the interactive elements makes the contrasting frustration all the more potent.

What is the most satisfying part of the interaction to you and what makes that part satisfying?

The most satisfying part of the experience is the tree that drops leaves when you mouseover it. I believe part of the reason it's so satisfying is that it involves a more sustained interactivity - the tree stretches and moves to follow your mouse as well as dropping leaves, so it's not just a single discrete action like a mouse click. The graphic is also pretty, and it has a tangible, tactile quality. The way the tree follows your mouse, it feels like you (the user) have a higher degree of control - like you're enacting the effect yourself, rather than just triggering a chain reaction, as with a single mouse click. It gives you a stronger sense of agency and immediacy. That's very satisfying.
